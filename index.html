Blue & White Ceramics


These porcelains from the collections of the Freer Gallery are part of a 1,500-year-old tradition of making porcelains in Jingdezhen, China. Porcelain production during the Kangxi period (1662–1722) expanded China’s export trade with Europe, sparked the Chinamania craze in the nineteenth century, and bolstered the East-West exchange that endures to this day.

Cobalt Blue Designs in Jingdezhen Ware

The sources, which are museum records of Chinese porcelain objects, frequently mention the use of cobalt blue decoration on Jingdezhen ware. The descriptions provide insights into how this distinctive colour was employed in the designs:


  • Underglaze Application: The cobalt pigment was applied under the glaze, as seen in the descriptions of the beaker-shaped vase, bottle-shaped vase, and incense burner. This technique involves painting the design onto the unglazed porcelain body before it is coated with a clear glaze and fired. The result is a durable and vibrant blue decoration protected beneath the glaze.
  • Landscapes and Motifs: The incense burner's description reveals how cobalt blue was used to depict detailed landscape scenes. These scenes included elements such as rocks, trees, water, figures, boats, and pavilions. Additionally, the beaker-shaped vase features a court scene, indicating that cobalt blue was also used to portray figures and narratives.
  • Stylistic Conventions: The incense burner's description highlights the use of parallel horizontal lines to represent water, a technique typical of the Kangxi period. This suggests that specific stylistic conventions were followed in applying the cobalt blue designs during different periods.
  • Bands and Borders: Cobalt blue was also used to create decorative bands and borders. The incense burner features a band around its neck composed of five of the Eight Precious Things alternating with cloud scrolls. The beaker-shaped vase incorporates herringbone borders to divide different sections of its decoration.
